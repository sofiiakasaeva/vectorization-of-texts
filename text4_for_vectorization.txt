Singapore's Changi Airport is famous for its waterfall, butterfly garden and seamless transit. But its real competitive edge is behind the scenes.
You've just landed after an 18-hour flight, red-eyed and yawning, and brace yourself for the usual airport indignities: the trudge to immigration, the dead-eyed queueing and the endless wait for your bag. Instead, smiling AI-powered autonomous cleaners wipe the spotless floors and immigration moves with the kind of speed that makes you momentarily suspicious. In less than 15 minutes, you're curbside in the tropical heat, wondering why the rest of the world still makes this so hard.
Days later, after a seamless check-in for your trip back home, you wait for your flight in the airport's transit halls, where there's a free 24/7 cinema, butterfly garden and the world's tallest indoor waterfall. There's even an indoor glass fishpond you can walk over, complete with a digital roof display that mirrors the weather outside. At times you forget you're in an airport, and not a tiny, futuristic city that's exceedingly well-run.
This may sound like a frequent flier's fever dream. In fact it's Singapore's Changi Airport, which has just won the Skytrax Award for World's Best Airport for the second year in a row, and 14 times overall.
Where other major airport terminals struggle with rodent infestations, crew strikes and collapsing ceilings, Changi's futuristic peacefulness feels a world apart. The gulf between an average and world-class airport has never felt larger. So, what does it take to make modern air travel run this smoothly - and how does Singapore keep getting it right when its rivals struggle?
Efficiency first, spectacle second
For Max Hirsh, managing director of Airport City Academy, a research centre focused on airport planning and development, Changi's success is not just about quality, but nailing the everyday fundamentals, from speed to safety to connectivity. It's also about having the flexibility to adapt when things don't go as planned.
"In the world of aviation, that happens a lot," Hirsh says. "The challenge isn't achieving that balance just once - it's maintaining it for decades in the face of changing demands, technologies and disruptions. Changi succeeds because it treats that balance as an ongoing project, not a one-off design feat."
If you've ever flown through Singapore, you'll likely have noticed the sense of calm that pervades the airport. What you likely don't realise is how carefully manufactured that tranquility is. Behind the scenes sits a huge and tightly choreographed operation in which automation, biometrics and predictive analytics are used to remove bottlenecks before they become visible, while 60,000 staff keep baggage, cleaning, energy use and passenger flow ticking along in sync. As Hirsh puts it, Changi often feels "one step ahead of you".
The same logic extends to the less glamorous details. Back-end groundwork like intuitive wayfinding, clear signage and crowd management means jet-lagged passengers aren't burning their cognitive load just trying to reach their gate. Five hundred toilets spread across the terminals doesn't hurt either. Each one comes with a digital touchscreen for passengers to rate their experience � any dip in ratings and a janitorial crew swoops down in minutes. "The hierarchy is simple," says Hirsh. "Efficiency first, atmosphere second, spectacle third."
